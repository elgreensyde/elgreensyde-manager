3. Cultivation Pipelines
The system employs a Three-Tiered Pipeline to accommodate all crop formats at Elgreensyde. Every crop lifecycle begins in a Tray and graduates to either a Plot (gram-based) or Batch (unit-based) depending on how it is sold.

4.5 Gram-Based Product Handling
When a gram-format product is added to cart, a weight entry modal fires. User enters weight in grams. System calculates price as weight multiplied by price per gram. Deduction from inventory is exact gram amount entered.

10.5 Recommendation Query Logic
When a checklist is submitted the system runs:
Step 1 — Filter by crop_id and variety
Step 2 — Filter by current growth_stage
Step 3 — Match trigger_conditions against checklist answers using AND or OR logic
Step 4 — Rank matched records by severity_level — Critical first
Step 5 — Run withholding cross-check against estimated harvest date
Step 6 — Run inputs availability check against inputs_inventory
Step 7 — Run cross-bed pattern detection against recent submissions
Step 8 — Display recommendation with all adjustments applied
10.6 Multiple Issues Handling
When multiple records match simultaneously the system displays a combined recommendation screen listing all issues in severity order with sequencing guidance: Address issues in severity order. Treating nutrient deficiency while disease is active is ineffective.

11. Smart Lifecycle Scheduler
Cascading timeline system that automatically calculates every downstream date from the sowing date. Any date change triggers a full recalculation of all subsequent dates and updates the task queue in real time.
11.1 Timeline Chain
Event

Cascade Rule: If any date in the chain is delayed — by weather, workload, or manual adjustment — every subsequent date shifts forward by the same number of days. The entire task queue updates automatically.
11.2 Scheduling Decision Chain
Before assigning any task to a date the system checks five conditions in order:
Step 1 — Is the day available? Sunday blocked. Away Period blocked. 5-task daily limit reached — move to next day.
Step 2 — Is the weather suitable? Pull Open-Meteo 7-day forecast. Apply crop-specific or universal weather thresholds.
Step 3 — Is the crop ready? Check timeline chain dates against today.
Step 4 — Are there conflicts? Transplanting and heavy harvesting same day — spread. Fertilizing before forecast rain — move. Spraying on windy day — move.
Step 5 — Assign to earliest passing day and add to task queue.

12. Harvest Safety Assessment
Runs automatically before any harvest is logged on a plot or batch. Triggered by flags from daily or weekly monitoring, or by system-detected conditions.
12.1 Pre-Harvest Check Sequence
Step 1 — Withholding period check: Last treatment date + product withholding days vs today. If today is within withholding period — harvest blocked with explanation.
Step 2 — Disease status check: Any open disease flags on this plot from last checklist? If yes — triggered checklist required before proceeding.
Step 3 — Pest status check: Any open pest flags? Severity determines response — mild triggers checklist, severe blocks harvest.
Step 4 — Plant health check: Any collapsed or dead plant flags? Partial harvest option presented.
Step 5 — Weather check: Current humidity above 85%? Warning displayed — foliar disease spread risk elevated post-harvest.
12.2 Harvest Outcomes

12.4 Edible Flower Safety Escalation
For edible flower crops (Sweet William, French Marigold, Torenia, Blue Ternate, Globe Amaranth) the harvest safety assessment has additional mandatory checks:
Any Botrytis spotting on blooms — harvest of affected blooms blocked, immediate removal and destroy task generated
Any pesticide application on blooms — bloom harvest blocked, pesticide-free zone violation flag raised
Last neem oil application within 3 days — harvest blocked, 3-day withholding enforced

Issue
Downy Mildew Preventive Protocol — Peronospora belbahrii
Cause Explanation
Downy mildew thrives in cool humid low-light conditions. Cloudy and rainy weather creates perfect spore germination environment. Semi-clay soil retains moisture longer than sandy soil elevating humidity at plant level. Enclosed nursery concentrates humidity around all trays simultaneously. Preventive action is critical before visible symptoms appear.
Action Steps
Step 1: Apply potassium bicarbonate spray as preventive coat on all active basil trays and beds. Mix at 4g per 1 liter (1 teaspoon per liter). Step 2: Spray thoroughly covering upper and lower leaf surfaces until runoff. Step 3: Apply in early morning so leaves dry before evening — wet leaves overnight dramatically increases infection risk. Step 4: Delay any foliar treatment if morning fog is present — wait until midday when leaf cuticle is fully dry.
Follow-up
Reapply every 5-7 days while cloudy or rainy conditions persist. Improve airflow by spacing trays further apart where possible. Avoid overhead watering — water at soil level only.

Downy Mildew Stage 1 — Early Detection
Cause Explanation
Pale yellowing on upper leaf surface is caused by the pathogen colonizing leaf tissue from the underside, disrupting chlorophyll production. At this stage sporulation has not yet appeared meaning the infection is still contained within the leaf tissue and has not begun releasing airborne spores. This is the most treatable stage. Day 3: Inspect all treated plants. Any new yellowing patches? If yes escalate to DM-003. Day 5: Reapply potassium bicarbonate if conditions remain cloudy or humid. Day 7: Full weekly checklist on all affected and neighboring beds.

Issue
Downy Mildew Stage 2 — Active Sporulation
Cause Explanation
Sporulation has begun. The pathogen has completed its initial infection cycle and is now actively producing and releasing airborne spores into the farm environment. Each sporulating lesion can release thousands of spores spreading to neighboring plants through air movement and water splash. The enclosed nursery environment and semi-clay soil moisture retention make escalation rapid under rainy or cloudy conditions. Potassium bicarbonate alone is no longer sufficient — systemic fungicide is needed to stop internal pathogen progression while potassium bicarbonate manages surface spore load.
Action Steps
Step 1: Remove and destroy all visibly affected plants immediately. Do not delay — every hour of sporulation means more spores in the farm environment. Step 2: Conduct emergency inspection of every active basil tray and bed on the farm regardless of distance from original detection. Downy mildew spores travel by air. Step 3: Apply systemic fungicide on all remaining basil plants farm-wide. Follow label rate precisely — reduce rate for Lemon Basil to prevent burn. Check withholding period against current harvest schedule before applying. Step 4: Apply potassium bicarbonate spray 24 hours after fungicide to address surface spore load. Do not mix together — apply separately. Step 5: Suspend overhead watering completely. Water at soil level only until outbreak is controlled. Step 6: If nursery trays affected, isolate entire nursery from bed area to prevent cross-contamination.
Follow-up
Day 2: Apply potassium bicarbonate as follow-up surface treatment. Day 3: Inspect all plants — any new sporulation on previously clean plants? Day 5: Reapply fungicide if new infections still appearing. Day 7: Full farm-wide weekly checklist. Day 14: If controlled, return to DM-001 preventive protocol.
Withholding Period
Check specific fungicide label. Do not harvest from treated beds until withholding period complete. Harvest from untreated beds safe if no symptoms.
Product
Systemic Fungicide (check label) followed by Potassium Bicarbonate 4g per liter 24 hours later

Trigger Conditions
Yellowing present AND location = lower older leaves AND pattern = uniform whole leaf yellowing not between veins AND one of: days since last urea 10 or more days, soil waterlogged 3 or more consecutive days, recent heavy rain period
Issue
Nitrogen Deficiency
Cause Explanation
Nitrogen is a mobile nutrient — the plant moves it from older lower leaves to newer growth when supply is insufficient. This is why yellowing always appears on lower older leaves first. On your farm two conditions cause deficiency even with weekly urea. First: prolonged waterlogging in semi-clay creates anaerobic conditions blocking nitrogen uptake regardless of soil urea levels. Second: heavy rain leaches water-soluble urea out of the root zone faster than roots absorb it. Cow manure and vermicast provide background organic nitrogen but cannot compensate quickly during peak vegetative demand.
Action Steps
Step 1: Confirm soil moisture. If waterlogged do not apply urea yet — it will not be absorbed and will leach. Improve drainage first and wait until soil returns to moist. Step 2: If soil moisture is normal apply urea drench immediately at 5g per liter of water. Apply evenly at soil level — avoid direct contact with leaves. Step 3: For faster recovery apply foliar urea at 2g per liter in early morning sprayed directly on leaves. Rinse leaves with plain water 30 minutes after foliar application. Step 4: Review watering schedule if waterlogging contributed.
Follow-up
Day 3: Yellowing should stabilize. New growth should appear healthy green. Day 5: If no improvement check soil pH — high or low pH locks out nitrogen. Ideal pH for basil is 6.0-7.0. Day 7: Resume normal weekly urea schedule if recovery confirmed.
Withholding Period
Zero days — rinse harvested leaves thoroughly
Product
Urea 46-0-0 at 5g per liter drench or 2g per liter foliar
Variety Flag
Lemon Basil: Reduce drench to 3g per liter, foliar to 1.5g per liter maximum to prevent burn

Issue
Phosphorus Lockout
Cause Explanation
Phosphorus lockout means phosphorus is present in the soil but the plant cannot access it. Bukidnon Adtuyon clay has natural acidity at pH 4.8-5.6. At pH below 6.0 phosphorus binds tightly to clay and iron particles becoming chemically unavailable to roots regardless of how much complete fertilizer is applied. Cool temperatures also slow root metabolic activity making phosphorus absorption difficult. The characteristic purple or reddish coloring on leaf undersides occurs as the plant accumulates sugars it cannot process without adequate phosphorus.
Action Steps
Step 1: Check soil pH if possible. Target range for basil is 6.0-7.0. Step 2: If pH below 6.0 apply agricultural lime or dolomite lime at bed edges without disturbing roots. Water in thoroughly. pH correction takes 1-2 weeks. Step 3: If pH is normal apply foliar complete fertilizer at half normal rate. Bypasses soil chemistry problem. Step 4: Improve drainage if waterlogging contributed — add CRH to bed surface top layer.
Follow-up
Day 7: Check for new growth color. Healthy new green leaves emerging is positive even if older leaves remain purple. Day 14: Recheck pH if lime applied. Day 21: Full weekly checklist to confirm recovery.
Withholding Period
Zero days
Product
Agricultural Lime or Dolomite Lime for pH correction. Complete Fertilizer 14-14-14 foliar at half rate.
Variety Flag
Thai Basil: Natural purple pigmentation on stems and edges — use stunted growth and small internode spacing as primary indicator rather than color alone

Issue
Potassium Deficiency
Cause Explanation
Potassium is a mobile nutrient responsible for water regulation, disease resistance, and structural integrity. Deficiency appears on older leaves first as the plant redirects potassium to new growth. The characteristic marginal chlorosis — scorched or yellowed leaf edges while center stays green — clearly distinguishes this from nitrogen deficiency which causes uniform whole-leaf yellowing. Most likely if urea has been applied exclusively without complete fertilizer for several weeks. Repeated heavy harvests deplete potassium faster than organic amendments can replace it.
Action Steps
Step 1: Apply complete fertilizer drench immediately to replenish potassium and phosphorus alongside nitrogen. Step 2: If moderate to severe supplement with potassium sulfate foliar spray at 3-5g per liter applied in early morning. Step 3: Review fertilizer rotation — reintroduce complete fertilizer on alternating weeks going forward. Step 4: After heavy harvests apply complete fertilizer within 2 days of cutting.
Follow-up
Day 5: Existing affected leaves will not recover but new growth should emerge without marginal symptoms. Day 7: Reapply complete fertilizer if moderate to severe. Day 14: Full checklist to confirm new growth is healthy.

Zero days — rinse leaves thoroughly before sale
Product
Complete Fertilizer 14-14-14 at standard label rate. Potassium Sulfate foliar at 3-5g per liter.

Issue
Fertilizer Burn
Cause Explanation
Fertilizer burn occurs when concentrated fertilizer salts contact plant tissue or when excessive soil fertilizer concentration draws water out of roots through osmosis. Most common causes on your farm: applying urea drench too close to stem base causing splash on lower leaves, applying during midday heat, or applying to very dry soil which concentrates the fertilizer solution around the root zone. Burn damage appears rapidly within 24-48 hours — the clearest timing indicator distinguishing it from other leaf damage.
Action Steps
Step 1: Water the bed thoroughly and immediately with plain water — at least double normal watering volume — to dilute fertilizer salt concentration. Step 2: If fertilizer contacted leaves directly rinse entire plant gently with plain water. Step 3: Do not apply additional fertilizer for 5-7 days. Step 4: Remove severely burned leaves if more than 50% of leaf surface is damaged. Step 5: Provide shade if burn occurred during a hot period.
Prevention
Always water bed before applying fertilizer drench — moist soil dilutes solution evenly. Apply fertilizer in early morning or late afternoon only — never midday. Direct drench at soil level between plants — never over leaves. Maintain 5g per liter maximum for Sweet and Thai Basil.
Follow-up
Day 2: Burned tissue will not recover but new growth should be unaffected. Day 5: Resume normal fertilizer schedule at standard rate with correct technique. Day 7: Full weekly checklist.
Withholding Period
Zero days — unaffected portions safe to harvest after thorough washing
Product
Flush with water — no product applied as treatment

Issue
Fertilizer Burn — Lemon Basil Specific
Cause Explanation
Lemon Basil has significantly thinner and more delicate leaf tissue than Sweet or Thai Basil making it disproportionately sensitive to fertilizer salts. Burn can occur at rates completely safe for other varieties. The thinner cuticle provides less protection against chemical contact damage. Recommended Rates

Step 4: Do not apply any synthetic fertilizer for minimum 7 days after burn event.
